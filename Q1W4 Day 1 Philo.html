Stage 2 (30 min): Defining the Terms & Claim Classifier

Key Distinctions: Fact vs. Opinion

FACT / TRUTH

  • Objective: Independent of individual feelings.
  • Verifiable: Can be proven/disproven using evidence (empirical or logical).
  • Universal: Generally accepted as true for everyone.
  • The Test: Verifiability.

OPINION

  • Subjective: Based on personal feelings, beliefs, or perspective.
  • Open to Debate: Cannot be conclusively proven or disproven.
  • Non-Universal: True only for the individual holding the belief.
  • Clue: Often uses phrases like "I feel," "I think," "should."

Discussion Point: Visualize the overlap and differences using a Venn Diagram.

The Path from Opinion to Truth

1. How Opinions are Formed

Opinions are fundamentally subjective claims influenced by internal and external factors:

  • Personal Experience: What you have seen, felt, or done (e.g., "The food at this restaurant is the best").
  • Cultural or Religious Beliefs: Core values learned from family or society (e.g., beliefs on morality or aesthetics).
  • Personal Preference/Emotion: Subjective feelings (e.g., "Blue is the prettiest color").
  • Insufficient Evidence: Drawing a conclusion before all facts are gathered (e.g., initial hypotheses).

2. The Transition to Fact (Verification)

An opinion can only become a universally accepted fact by passing the rigorous test of Verifiability, backed by sufficient evidence:

  • Empirical Evidence (Scientific): Gathered through observation, measurement, and experimentation (e.g., clinical trial results, astronomical measurements).
  • Logical/Mathematical Proof: Verified through formal systems where conclusions necessarily follow from premises (e.g., $1+1=2$, geometric proofs).
  • Testimonial Evidence (Historical): Verified through reliable primary and secondary sources and corroborated accounts (e.g., historical documents).
  • Statistical Evidence: Verified through objective data and quantitative analysis (e.g., census data, unemployment rates).

The rigor of verification is what separates personal belief from objective knowledge.

Stage 3 (15 min): Practice - Opinion-to-Truth Challenge

SLO: Identify what kind of verifiable evidence is necessary to elevate a subjective opinion into a factual claim.

Group Challenge: Verifying Claims

Take this common opinion:

"Smartphones are making the current generation less focused."

Task (Pairs): Brainstorm one specific, verifiable, and objective piece of evidence that would be required to transform this statement from a mere opinion into a widely accepted factual truth.

  • Focus: What methodology (e.g., peer-reviewed study, standardized test data, brain scans) is required?
  • Requirement: Must be something measurable, not a feeling or anecdotal evidence.

Teacher Notes/Prompt:

Facilitate group discussion. Guide students towards empirical evidence, for example: "A controlled neuroscientific study comparing average attention spans (measured in seconds) of 100 students before smartphone ownership was common vs. 100 students today, using identical focus tasks."

Stage 4 (5 min): Conclusion & Exit Slip

Recap of the core concept: Verifiability is the key distinction between subjective claims and objective reality. It is the filter we use when doing philosophy.
