I. The Branches of Philosophy

Logic

The "Science of Correct Thinking."

Scenario: A student identifies that a speaker is attacking an opponent’s character (Ad Hominem) rather than the actual argument.

Epistemology

Sources of knowledge (Rationalism vs. Empiricism).

Scenario: A researcher asks: "Is a single social media poll a reliable source of knowledge for an entire country?"

Metaphysics

Nature of reality and the mind-body connection.

Scenario: Debating if your "Self" remains the same if your memories are uploaded into a mechanical computer body.

Aesthetics

The study of beauty and art.

Scenario: A city committee debates if a jagged steel sculpture is "beautiful" or an eyesore.

Ethics / Political Philosophy

Moral conduct and foundations of society.

Scenario: Deciding if it is just to spend a school budget on one event vs. splitting it for everyone.

II. Western Philosophy

From Mythos to Logos

The Shift

The Example: Greeks stopped blaming mountain spirits for volcanoes (Mythos) and began studying tectonic pressure (Logos).

Thales of Miletus

The first philosopher. Proposed the Arche is Water.

Scenario: Observing that life requires moisture and water changes states, connecting all nature via one substance.

The Athenian Giants

Philosopher Key Claim Scenario
Socrates Unexamined life; Socratic Method. Questioning "What is bravery?" until the student refines their definition.
Plato Idealism; World of Forms. No one can draw a "perfect line," but the Idea exists in the mind.
Aristotle Naturalism; The Golden Mean. Confidence is the balance between Arrogance and Insecurity.

IV. Truth vs. Opinion

Correspondence Theory

Scenario: Checking the fridge for milk. The statement "there is milk" is true only if the physical reality matches the words.

Coherence Theory

Scenario: A judge checks if a new law fits within the Constitutional system. If it contradicts, it is "false" in that system.

Pragmatic Theory

Scenario: A company adopts a new software. It is "true/best" because it practically finished the project faster.

V. Scientific vs. Philosophical Truth

Scientific (Provisional): A doctor says a drug works based on trials, but admits future data could change this conclusion.
Philosophical (Rational): Arguing "Humans deserve dignity" based on the logic of personhood, not on lab measurements.

VI. Ways of Doing Philosophy

Case Study: LOVE

1. Analysis

Defining Love by its parts: 1) Connection, 2) Vulnerability, and 3) Commitment.

2. Abstraction

Seeing a hug, a "thank you," and a mother feeding a child, then identifying the universal concept: Love.

3. Justification

Dilemma: Lying to a partner to save feelings.
• Consequentialism: Lie if the outcome is happy.
• Deontology: Be honest because lying is a broken rule.

4. Synthesis

Finding the "Third Way" by balancing Total Independence with Total Dependency to reach healthy partnership.
